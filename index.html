Breadth Area: Fine Arts & Humanities


FILMMAKING
  • My early career ambition was to be a storyteller. While most of my peers were starting their sophomore years in college, I packed up my car and went to Los Angeles, where a wave of independent filmmaking was overtaking Hollywood. Names like Spike Lee, Quentin Tarantino, and Kevin Smith had broken the mold set by such names as Scorsese, Spielberg, and Allen.  
  • I had hoped to gain experience there by working in film, but instead I found that there was more work in office environments, and that the successful filmmakers were few and far between, and thus, hard to introduce oneself to. Determined to learn though, I spent as much time as I could studying filmmaking by learning from whatever I could get my hands on, and by sneaking in to the Library of the American Film Institute to read books on the cinema and to watch films. 
  • Having had little luck in Los Angeles as a filmmaker in practice, I returned to the Midwest, determined to keep learning and trying to gain experience in the craft. Work as a copywriter for an advertising agency allowed me to be involved in creating television commercial content, as well as giving me access to the sets of shoots for local and regional clients. The agency position also gave me the opportunity to create print and radio advertising, but after 9/11 and with the advertising industry in a massive downtown, I was let go, and the agency eventually folded. Within a short time, my skills in business administration eventually landed me a spot helping to sell a video magazine at a small wedding video production house called August Productions. It was here where I finally had access to 16mm film cameras and Steenbeck editing equipment (which I spent more time and effort getting repaired than actually being able to do work with).
  • My first short film Making the Cosmopolitan, with a crew of 15 and a cast of five, was shot in one night and edited over the course of several months. I was the producer and director, and edited it as well. Making the Cosmopolitan afforded me the opportunity to work with skilled camerapeople, talented lighting technicians, and a professional costume designer, while the editing and sound design processes gave me the opportunity to work with voice-over talent and to negotiate for usage rights.
  • In 2003, Speak, a Showtime Original Movie starring Kristen Stewart of Twilight fame, came to town. I set out to make sure that I got a spot on the crew, and I did--just one of three full-time Columbus, Ohio residents on the crew of more than 50 people from New York, LA and Austin, TX. The role that I managed to hustle my way into was the craft services person, and on a film set, this position basically runs a snack bar. It was exhausting work, and I was often far away from where filming was. It was not a wash, however, as I got to know just about everyone on the set and became a sort of confidant to key members of the cast and crew. Above all, my hard work was noticed by many people who would stay in touch after the shoot was over.
  • After Speak, I was able to find plenty of film work on local and regional shoots. No more "crafty", either, as I served in capacities ranging from lighting and electric to assistant camera and sound. I was eventually recruited by a filmmaker in Athens in Ohio to produce her short film, a twenty minute piece which she wrote and would direct. Serving as the producer of the film and armed with a modest budget, I hired the crew and gear, enlisted students from Ohio University to help, and managed daily operations during the two week shoot. The film, Again, Love Beem, premiered at the 2005 Ohio International Film Festival.
  • Back at August Productions in Columbus, the constant state of disrepair of the film equipment to which I had access to was not necessarily a bad thing. Needing to fix the flatbed 16mm film editor helped me make fast friends with a cameraman with an arsenal of film equipment at his disposal. He took an interest in what I was trying to do, and offered to help whenever it was needed. I quickly took him up on his offer, and with a 35mm camera and a crew of 10, I was able to make The Atheist, a short film which was shot in a day, and edited over the course of a month.
  • The Atheist went on to play at a small upstart international film festival in Bethel, Connecticut, which would eventually become the home for The Connecticut Film Festival. I was flown in to attend the screening and took questions from the audience. It was also an excellent opportunity to network with other filmmakers.
  • In 2005, I was able to pull together a professional crew of 30 volunteers to produce Same Time..., a 5 minute short shot in one night on 16mm film. I took the film to Los Angeles to edit it, and worked with an amazing composer who had also composed a score for my short film The Atheist.
  • After moving to Chicago in unsuccessful pursuit of a film grant, I was asked to direct the concept film for a script based on a popular role-playing game, Brave New World. In total, I spent several months preparing the script, drawing up storyboards, arranging for meetings with the Chicago Film Office to lock down locations, and working with actors to prepare them for a rigorous one-day shoot.
  • All said, as an independent filmmaker, I have produced and/or directed seven short films, pulling together crews of 7 to 30 on little or no budget, while learning how to tell a story with a teams of knowledgeable and talented film and television personnel of every skill level.
